# Discussion

## Summary of Findings
This NMA synthesizes the most comprehensive evidence base for HIV PrEP to date,
including 14 RCTs, 50,743 participants, and 8 intervention nodes spanning the
full spectrum from 1990s-era oral tenofovir to twice-yearly injectable
lenacapavir. Several key findings emerge.

First, the long-acting injectables — lenacapavir and CAB-LA — rank clearly
superior to oral agents for HIV prevention, with efficacy estimates of 97% and
88% versus placebo respectively. This hierarchy is biologically plausible:
sustained drug levels achieved by long-acting formulations bypass the critical
vulnerability of daily pill adherence, which undermined TDF/FTC efficacy in
several early trials in women (FEM-PrEP and VOICE).^1 When adherence-confounded
trials are excluded in sensitivity analysis, oral TDF/FTC efficacy strengthens
to 67%, consistent with biologically complete drug exposure, but still falls
below long-acting benchmarks.

Second, the dapivirine vaginal ring, while the least efficacious node in the
network (31% reduction; SUCRA 57.3%), provides several features not captured
by efficacy rankings alone: it is covert, woman-controlled, requires no clinic
visit for routine use (self-inserted monthly), and carries no systemic absorption
profile that interferes with other medications.^2 For women in settings where
daily pills or bimonthly injections are logistically challenging or
socially untenable, the ring represents a meaningful public health tool.
Our subgroup analysis confirms the ring's consistent — if modest — efficacy
specifically in cisgender women in sub-Saharan Africa.

Third, TDF/FTC on-demand (the 2-1-1 strategy) achieves high SUCRA (79.8%) and
demonstrated 86% efficacy in IPERGAY among MSM, comparable to injectable
approaches.^3 However, on-demand dosing is biologically inappropriate for
cisgender women (inadequate mucosal TDF concentrations with coitally timed
dosing) and has only been validated in MSM with predictable exposure patterns.
Its ranking therefore must be interpreted in population context.

## Consistency with Prior Evidence
Our findings are consistent with prior pairwise analyses and the direction of
individual trial results. The DISCOVER trial showed non-inferiority of TAF/FTC
to TDF/FTC (both ~0.50 infections per 100 PY in MSM), reflected in our NMA
by similar SUCRA values (TAF/FTC 81.2% vs TDF/FTC 72.4%).^4 The superiority
of CAB-LA over TDF/FTC, established individually in HPTN 083 and HPTN 084,
is confirmed and quantified in network context. The apparent superiority of
lenacapavir over CAB-LA reached the point estimate threshold (IRR 0.27) but
the 95% CI crossed unity (0.04–1.64), reflecting uncertainty in this indirect
comparison — a finding that underscores the need for a direct lenacapavir vs
CAB-LA RCT.

## Limitations
Several limitations merit consideration. First, the network relies partially
on indirect comparisons: no trial has directly compared lenacapavir with CAB-LA
or dapivirine ring with any oral agent. Transitivity in these comparisons
requires the assumption that background HIV incidence, risk behaviour, and
concomitant prevention services are similar across studies — an assumption
that is imperfect given the varied geographic settings and populations.
Second, the heterogeneity in placebo-arm incidence rates (0.46 to 6.31 per
100 PY) across trials reflects real differences in underlying HIV risk, which
may moderate relative efficacy estimates. Third, the zero-event arm in PURPOSE 1
(lenacapavir) necessitated a continuity correction, introducing minor uncertainty
into the most efficacious estimate. Fourth, all long-acting injectable trials
used active comparators (TDF/FTC), meaning their efficacy vs placebo is an
indirect NMA estimate rather than a directly observed RCT finding.

## Implications for Practice and Policy
These findings support a tiered, patient-centred approach to PrEP choice.
For populations with high access to injectable services and the highest efficacy
requirement (e.g., serodiscordant couples, highly exposed MSM, young women in
high-prevalence settings), long-acting lenacapavir or CAB-LA should be preferred
where available. Daily oral TDF/FTC and TAF/FTC remain effective, accessible,
and cost-effective first-line options, particularly in resource-constrained
settings where the cost of long-acting agents remains prohibitive. The dapivirine
ring provides an important woman-controlled option for settings where oral pills
or injectables face adherence or social barriers. Costing analyses and
implementation research are urgently needed to guide scale-up decisions.

## Conclusion
All approved PrEP modalities reduce HIV incidence. Twice-yearly lenacapavir
ranks highest for efficacy among all options evaluated, followed by CAB-LA.
Long-acting injectables offer transformative protection by eliminating daily
adherence requirements. The dapivirine ring, while lower in relative efficacy,
addresses a distinct and underserved prevention niche. A direct head-to-head
trial of lenacapavir versus CAB-LA is the single most important evidence gap
to address in future PrEP research.
