# Introduction

HIV pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP) represents one of the most consequential
biomedical advances in the global HIV response. Since the iPrEx trial
demonstrated in 2010 that daily oral tenofovir disoproxil fumarate/emtricitabine
(TDF/FTC) reduces HIV acquisition among men who have sex with men (MSM) by 44%,
the PrEP landscape has undergone dramatic transformation.^1 The portfolio now
encompasses at least five mechanistically distinct modalities across three routes
of delivery: daily oral agents (TDF/FTC and tenofovir alafenamide/emtricitabine
[TAF/FTC]), an event-driven oral regimen (on-demand TDF/FTC, the "2-1-1" scheme),
long-acting intramuscular injectable cabotegravir (CAB-LA), twice-yearly
subcutaneous injectable lenacapavir, and the monthly dapivirine intravaginal ring.

Despite this proliferation of options, clinical and public health guidance
remains fragmented. Guidelines from the World Health Organization (WHO),
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), and regional health authorities
have generally evaluated each modality in isolation, based on its pivotal trial
versus placebo or an active comparator.^2,3 This approach obscures the relative
hierarchy of protection across modalities, particularly for the populations most
affected by HIV — cisgender women in sub-Saharan Africa, MSM, and transgender
women — who now have multiple PrEP options but lack clear evidence-based guidance
on comparative efficacy.

Several characteristics make a comprehensive NMA uniquely timely and feasible.
First, the network of direct comparisons has become substantially richer:
HPTN 083 and HPTN 084 compared CAB-LA with TDF/FTC in MSM/transgender women
and cisgender women respectively^4,5; PURPOSE 1 and PURPOSE 2 compared
lenacapavir against TDF/FTC (and TAF/FTC) in cisgender women and in MSM/
gender-diverse persons, with PURPOSE 1 reporting 100% efficacy for lenacapavir
(zero seroconversions among 2,134 participants).^6,7 Second, the dapivirine
vaginal ring, the first vaginal ring approved for HIV prevention (European
Medicines Agency Article 58 positive opinion 2020; WHO prequalified 2022),
provides a uniquely woman-controlled prevention option that has been compared
with placebo in two large trials (ASPIRE and Ring Study).^8,9 Third, lenacapavir
received FDA approval for PrEP in June 2025, completing a network that spans the
full spectrum of PrEP innovation.

Prior NMAs in HIV prevention have been limited in scope, focusing on adherence
interventions to oral PrEP rather than comparative efficacy across modalities,
or predating the long-acting era.^10 We therefore conducted a systematic review
and NMA to synthesize efficacy and safety across all currently approved and
investigational PrEP interventions. Our primary objective was to generate
comparative incidence rate ratios and SUCRA rankings to inform clinical
decision-making, guideline development, and health technology assessment.
